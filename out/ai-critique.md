# AI Critique

During this assignment, the biggest mistake made by the AI happened in the FR-08 task. Instead of staying within the assigned feature, it also modeled FR-09 (customer coupon application) because it assumed that coupons were naturally part of the checkout process. The reasoning sounded convincing, and it even generated a complete Decision Table and found several real defects. However, those defects were outside the assignment scope. I only noticed the problem because the amount of work seemed much larger than expected, so I went back and checked the assignment requirements. The AI had access to the official feature list from the beginning, but it relied on the previous conversation instead of verifying the scope directly from the document.

After that experience, I changed the workflow for the remaining features. Before starting any modeling or test design, I required the AI to identify the assigned feature, cite the corresponding requirement in the README, and confirm that every variable and rule belonged to that feature only. This simple verification step prevented the same mistake from happening again, especially for FR-17, which is closely related to the feature that caused the earlier confusion.

The most important lesson I learned is that AI can produce detailed and convincing results that are still based on an incorrect assumption. A well-written answer does not guarantee that it answers the right question. Instead of trusting the AI because its output looks complete, I should always verify the scope first and treat the official specification as the final authority. Careful review and clear checkpoints are much more reliable than simply asking the AI to "be more careful."
